# Decision Frameworks Reference

Frameworks used across IDKWTD skills. Skills reference specific frameworks
by name rather than re-explaining them each time.

---

## The Regret Test

Ask: "Imagine you're 80 years old looking back. Which choice would you
regret NOT making?"

Why it works: Fear of failure is present-tense. Regret is future-tense.
Shifting the time horizon bypasses the immediate anxiety and accesses
what actually matters to them.

Used in: /session (Decision Mode), /reality-check, /options-lab

---

## The Reversibility Matrix

Classify decisions by reversibility and stakes:

```
                    Low Stakes        High Stakes
Reversible          Just do it        Try it with a deadline
Irreversible        Think briefly     Think deeply
```

Most decisions people agonize over are in the "Reversible + High Stakes"
quadrant. The answer is almost always "try it with a deadline."

Used in: /reality-check, /session (Phase 4)

## The 10/10/10 Rule

For any decision, ask:
- How will I feel about this in 10 minutes?
- How will I feel about this in 10 months?
- How will I feel about this in 10 years?

The 10-minute answer is usually emotion. The 10-month answer is practical.
The 10-year answer reveals what matters. Weight the 10-year answer most.

Used in: /options-lab (Phase 4)

## The Pre-Mortem

Before committing to a path, imagine it failed completely. Then ask:
"What's the most likely reason it failed?"

This surfaces risks that optimism hides. It's not pessimism — it's
preparedness. The goal isn't to scare them out of acting. It's to
identify the specific risks they should plan for.

Used in: /options-lab (Phase 4), /session (Phase 4)

## The Energy Audit

For any option, ask: "Does thinking about executing this give you energy
or drain you?"

Energy is a better signal than logic for life direction decisions. People
can logic themselves into a decision they hate. Energy doesn't lie.

Caveat: Distinguish between "this drains me because it's wrong for me"
and "this drains me because it's hard." Hard things that matter often
feel draining but also compelling. Things that are wrong for you feel
draining and empty.

Used in: /options-lab, /session

## The Constraint Stack

Apply constraints in order to filter options:

1. **Survival constraints** — What do I need to stay alive and housed?
2. **Obligation constraints** — What must I do for people who depend on me?
3. **Skills constraints** — What can I actually do right now?
4. **Values constraints** — What aligns with what I care about?
5. **Energy constraints** — What gives me energy vs. drains me?

Each level eliminates options. By level 5, you should have 2-4 options
left. If you have zero, loosen the constraints from the bottom up.

Used in: /session (Phase 4), /options-lab (Phase 3)

## The Two-List Method

When someone has too many options:

1. Write every option on a list (the "everything" list)
2. Circle only the ones they'd be genuinely excited to start tomorrow
3. The uncircled items go on a "NOT NOW" list

The NOT NOW list is not a rejection. It's a parking lot. The circled
items are the real candidates.

Used in: /options-lab (Phase 2), /session (Filter Mode)

## The Identity Separation

When someone is stuck because of who they think they are:

Ask: "If you had zero reputation, zero history, and nobody knew your
name — what would you choose?"

This separates the decision from identity maintenance. People often
stay on wrong paths because leaving would require them to admit they
were wrong, or because their social identity is tied to the path.

Used in: /fear-audit (Identity Fear), /session (Escape Mode)

## The Dependency Map

For any option, list everything that must go right for it to work.
Count the dependencies:

- 0-2 dependencies: High agency. You control the outcome.
- 3-5 dependencies: Moderate. Some things are outside your control.
- 6+ dependencies: Low agency. Too many things must align. Risky.

Prefer options with fewer dependencies, especially for first moves.

Used in: /options-lab (Phase 4)

## The Minimum Viable Experiment

For any direction, ask: "What's the smallest possible version you could
test in one week, with zero financial commitment?"

Examples:
- Want to freelance? Do one free project this week.
- Want to teach? Explain something to someone and get feedback.
- Want to change industries? Have three conversations with people in
  that industry.
- Want to build a product? Make a landing page and share it.
- Want to move cities? Spend a weekend there and imagine daily life.

The experiment generates information. Information reduces uncertainty.
Reduced uncertainty enables better decisions.

Used in: All skills. This is the most important framework in IDKWTD.

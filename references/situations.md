# Situation Handling Reference

This file contains expanded guidance for specific life situations that
IDKWTD skills may encounter. Skills reference this file when they need
deeper context for a particular scenario.

---

## Career Situations

### "I just got laid off"

**Emotional state:** Shock, anger, shame, relief (sometimes all at once).
The shame is often the loudest but least acknowledged.

**Common mistakes:**
- Immediately applying to 50 jobs out of panic (spray and pray)
- Telling themselves "this is actually a good thing" before processing
- Isolating because they feel embarrassed
- Making big decisions in the first 72 hours

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Acknowledge the loss without rushing past it (but don't marinate)
2. Stabilize: What's the financial runway? (/money-map)
3. Separate identity from job title: "You lost your role, not your skills"
4. Wait 1 week before committing to any direction
5. Use the gap to do /skill-scan and /network-map — things they never had
   time for while employed

### "AI is going to take my job"

**Emotional state:** Existential dread mixed with uncertainty about what's
real vs. hype.

**Common mistakes:**
- Believing every headline without examining their specific role
- Ignoring it entirely ("it won't affect me")
- Panic-pivoting to "AI-proof" careers without understanding what that means

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Separate signal from noise: "What do you actually do day-to-day?"
2. Assess which specific tasks are automatable NOW vs. in theory
3. Identify the human elements of their work that resist automation
4. Reframe: "AI doesn't replace people. It replaces tasks. Which of your
   tasks are at risk, and which ones become MORE valuable?"
5. If real risk exists, start building adjacent skills now while employed

### "I hate my job but it pays well"

**Emotional state:** Golden handcuffs. Guilt about complaining when others
have it worse. Slow erosion of identity.

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Validate: "The fact that it pays well doesn't mean you're wrong to hate it"
2. Map the REAL cost of staying: health, relationships, time, opportunity
3. Calculate what "enough" actually is (/money-map) — often less than they think
4. Find the specific thing they hate: the work itself, the culture, the
   manager, the mission, the monotony? These have different solutions.
5. Explore bridging strategies: can they change roles internally? Go part-time?
   Build something on the side while employed?

### "I want to switch careers but I'm too old"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Challenge the assumption: "Too old for what, specifically?"
2. Distinguish between genuine age constraints (professional athlete at 45)
   and imagined ones (software engineer at 35)
3. Reframe experience as leverage, not liability: "You have 15 years of
   [skills] that a 22-year-old doesn't. That's not a disadvantage."
4. Find examples of successful transitions at their age (web search)
5. Start with the smallest possible experiment, not a full career change

### "I have a chronic illness and I'm trying to figure out my career"

**Emotional state:** Exhaustion mixed with guilt. Feels behind peers. Energy
is limited and unpredictable. Every career framework assumes full health.

**Common mistakes:**
- Comparing to healthy peers
- Ignoring energy as a constraint
- Taking jobs that trigger flare-ups
- Not disclosing when they should, or over-disclosing

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Treat energy as the primary constraint — more important than money or
   prestige
2. Map energy patterns: what time of day, what activities, what environments
3. Build career around energy peaks, not around what "should" be possible
4. Constraints are features

### "Golden handcuffs — RSUs, pension, deferred comp"

**Emotional state:** Trapped but comfortable. Knows they should leave but the
financial cost feels enormous. Feels guilty for complaining about being
well-paid.

**Common mistakes:**
- Only counting what they'd lose, not what they'd gain
- Treating unvested compensation as guaranteed
- Staying "one more year" repeatedly
- Conflating financial security with happiness

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Calculate the ACTUAL number: what's the walk-away cost? Often smaller
   than imagined
2. Use the Reversibility Matrix — is this truly irreversible?
3. Map the cost of staying (health, relationships, years of your life)
   against the cost of leaving
4. Name the sunk cost fallacy directly

### "Post-military transition"

**Emotional state:** Identity loss. Skills don't translate to civilian
language. Structure suddenly gone. May feel simultaneously overqualified
and underqualified.

**Common mistakes:**
- Underselling military experience
- Only looking at obvious transitions (security, defense contractors)
- Isolating from civilian networks
- Missing the loss of identity and community, not just the job

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Translate skills into civilian evidence: "managed logistics for 200
   people" not "served in the military"
2. Map the identity transition separately from the career transition —
   they're different problems
3. Use /network-map to build civilian connections

### "First-generation — no family playbook"

**Emotional state:** Making it up as they go. No one in their family has
done this before. Torn between family expectations and unfamiliar
opportunities. Imposter syndrome amplified.

**Common mistakes:**
- Not asking for help because they don't know what to ask
- Accepting the first opportunity instead of evaluating options
- Feeling disloyal for wanting something different
- No mentorship model

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Normalize the disorientation — this IS harder without a playbook
2. Use /network-map specifically to find people one step ahead (not five
   steps ahead)
3. Focus on building the map they don't have rather than following one
   that doesn't exist

### "Returning to work after years away"

**Emotional state:** Confidence gap. Skills feel outdated. Identity has
shifted (from professional to parent/caregiver and back). Worried about
explaining the gap.

**Common mistakes:**
- Apologizing for the gap
- Trying to return to the exact same role/level
- Ignoring skills gained during time away (managing a household IS project
  management)
- Undervaluing themselves in negotiations

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Use /skill-scan to inventory skills gained during time away — they're
   real skills
2. Reframe the gap as a choice, not a failure
3. Target roles that value the combination of prior experience + life
   experience
4. Don't aim for where you left off; aim for where your skills point now

### "Mid-career burnout vs. wrong career"

**Emotional state:** Can't tell if they hate THIS job or THE job. Exhausted.
Fantasizing about completely different lives. Dreading Monday but not sure
if switching companies or switching careers is the answer.

**Common mistakes:**
- Quitting in the burnout trough
- Assuming burnout = wrong career (sometimes it's wrong company, wrong
  manager, wrong workload)
- Making permanent decisions from temporary states
- Not taking recovery time before deciding

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Separate the variables: Is it the work itself, the environment, the
   people, or the pace? The Energy Audit framework helps here.
2. If they've been burnt out for 6+ months, suggest recovery before any
   career decision
3. "Don't make a permanent decision from a temporary state"

## Life Situations

### "I'm going through a breakup/divorce"

**Emotional state:** Grief, anger, loss of shared identity, practical chaos.

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Acknowledge that this affects EVERYTHING, not just relationships
2. Do NOT try to solve the career/life direction problem immediately
3. Focus on stabilization: housing, finances, support system
4. Set a timeline: "For the next [X weeks], your only job is to get stable.
   Direction comes after stability."
5. Suggest professional support (therapist, counselor) for the emotional work
6. /money-map is often urgent here — shared finances are splitting

### "I have a family to support and can't take risks"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Respect the constraint completely. Don't romanticize risk-taking.
2. Redefine "risk": Small experiments while employed are not risky. Quitting
   with no plan is risky. These are different things.
3. Find the 5-10 hours per week they can reallocate (audit their time)
4. Build proof of concept while maintaining stability
5. Set a trigger point: "When [measurable milestone], then [bigger move]"

### "Everyone around me seems to have it figured out"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Name the illusion directly: "They don't. You're comparing your inside
   to their outside."
2. If possible, ask: "Do you know anyone well enough to know their real
   situation? Are they actually happy or just appearing successful?"
3. Redirect to their own signal: "Other people's paths are noise for your
   decision. What matters is YOUR values, YOUR constraints, YOUR strengths."

### "My parents want me to do X"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Separate love from direction: "Your parents love you. That doesn't mean
   their career advice is right for you."
2. Understand the cultural context. In many cultures, family expectations
   carry real weight and ignoring them has real social consequences.
3. Don't dismiss the family voice, but don't let it be the only voice:
   "What do YOUR values say? If family opinion were removed, what would
   you choose?"
4. Find the overlap: sometimes what parents want and what the person wants
   are closer than they think, just framed differently
5. If genuine conflict: help them articulate their position to their family
   with respect and clarity

### "Caring for aging parents"

**Emotional state:** Sandwich generation pressure. Career decisions now have
someone else's wellbeing attached. Guilt about wanting their own life.
Siblings may not be sharing the load equally.

**Common mistakes:**
- Sacrificing career entirely without exploring alternatives
- Not having honest conversations with siblings
- Ignoring their own burnout
- Making assumptions about what parents actually want

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Map the actual caregiving load: hours per week, tasks, who else helps
2. Use /money-map to understand the financial picture (parents' resources +
   their own)
3. This is a constraint to build around, not a reason to stop building
4. Name the resentment if it exists — it's useful information

### "Grief — someone close died"

**Emotional state:** Nothing makes sense. Priorities have shifted but they
don't know to what. May feel urgency to change everything or paralysis to
change nothing. Previous goals may feel meaningless.

**Common mistakes:**
- Making big decisions while actively grieving
- Using busyness to avoid grief
- Assuming grief has a timeline
- Ignoring how loss reshapes values

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. If grief is fresh (< 3 months), this is NOT the time for a full /session.
   Acknowledge the loss directly.
2. Suggest they focus on stability, not direction
3. If grief is older but still reshaping them, use Phase 2 values extraction
   — grief often clarifies what actually matters
4. Be careful: IDKWTD is not grief counseling

### "Immigration / visa constraints"

**Emotional state:** Career options filtered by legal status. May be highly
skilled but constrained to specific employers or industries. Feels like
playing the game on hard mode. Uncertainty about long-term status adds
anxiety to every decision.

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting bad conditions because "at least I have a visa"
- Not exploring all legal options
- Comparing to people without visa constraints
- Planning only for the visa timeline, not their actual life

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Visa status is a constraint — treat it as a filter, not a cage
2. Map what IS possible within the constraint, not what isn't
3. Use /money-map to understand the financial safety net needed
4. Connect with others who've navigated the same system via /network-map

## Psychological Patterns

### Analysis Paralysis

**Signs:** They've been "thinking about it" for months. They consume
information but don't act. Every conversation adds options instead of
eliminating them.

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Name it: "You're not gathering information anymore. You're using
   research as a way to avoid deciding."
2. Set a deadline: "You will decide by [date]. Not the perfect decision.
   A decision."
3. Use the "two-envelope" technique: "If you had to choose between only
   these two options, right now, which one?" Force binary choices.
4. Reframe the cost: "Every week you spend analyzing, you're paying the
   cost of inaction. What has that cost you so far?"

### Imposter Syndrome

**Signs:** They have skills and experience but don't believe they're
qualified. They attribute success to luck. They fear being "found out."

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Don't reassure. Show evidence. "You said people come to you for [X].
   That's not luck. That's competence."
2. Separate feeling from fact: "You FEEL like you don't belong. What's
   the EVIDENCE? Have you been fired? Have people complained? Or is this
   a story you're telling yourself?"
3. Use /skill-scan to build an evidence-based view of their abilities
4. Reframe: "Imposter syndrome is most common in competent people. The
   truly incompetent don't worry about being found out."

### Sunk Cost Attachment

**Signs:** "But I've already invested X years/dollars/effort in this path."

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Name the fallacy directly: "The time and money you've spent are gone
   regardless of what you do next. The question is: what's the best use
   of the time and money you have LEFT?"
2. Reframe the investment: "Those X years gave you [skills/knowledge/
   connections]. Those don't disappear when you change direction."
3. Calculate the forward cost: "If you stay for 5 more years out of sunk
   cost, what does that cost you?"

### Perfectionism Disguised as Planning

**Signs:** "I need to have everything figured out before I start." They
won't act until the plan is "perfect."

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Name it: "You're not planning. You're using planning as a shield
   against the discomfort of uncertainty."
2. Challenge the premise: "Has a plan ever survived contact with reality?
   The fastest way to get information is to act, not to plan more."
3. Use minimum viable experiments: "What's the smallest thing you can do
   to test this WITHOUT a complete plan?"
4. Set the quality bar explicitly: "70% sure is enough to start. You'll
   learn more from one week of doing than one month of planning."

### Neurodivergent Decision-Making

**Signs:** Standard career advice doesn't fit. May have high capability in
specific areas but struggle with things others find easy. Executive function
challenges make "just take the first step" advice useless. Hyperfocus can
be a superpower or a trap.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forcing neurotypical frameworks
- Ignoring energy/executive function as real constraints
- Not leveraging hyperfocus areas
- Internalizing "lazy" narratives

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Redesign the assignment structure: break it into smaller steps with
   external accountability
2. Map energy and focus patterns — when do they have capacity?
3. Build around peaks. Treat executive function as a constraint like money
   — work with it, not against it.
4. The 48-hour assignment may need to be a 24-hour micro-action

## Financial Situations

### "I have debt and it's controlling my decisions"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Get the numbers (/money-map). Vague debt fear is always worse than
   specific debt knowledge.
2. Separate "debt as obstacle" from "debt as excuse": Is the debt
   genuinely preventing action, or is it a convenient reason not to act?
3. For genuine debt constraints: build a minimum viable income plan first
4. For debt as excuse: "Your debt is [amount]. Your monthly payment is
   [amount]. That constrains your options, but it doesn't eliminate them.
   What can you do WHILE paying down debt?"

### "I can't afford to take a pay cut"

**IDKWTD approach:**
1. Challenge "can't afford": "What does your actual budget look like?"
   (/money-map). Often there's more flexibility than they think.
2. Calculate the real number: "What's the MINIMUM you need to cover
   non-negotiable expenses?"
3. Explore bridging strategies: freelancing, part-time work, consulting
   in their current field while building the new thing
4. Reframe: "Taking a temporary pay cut is an investment, not a loss.
   What's the expected return?"

## Crisis Detection

If any of these appear, pause the workflow and prioritize safety:

- Mentions of self-harm or suicidal thoughts
- Severe hopelessness beyond normal frustration
- Descriptions of abuse or unsafe living situations
- Signs of severe depression (can't get out of bed, not eating, isolation)
- Substance abuse as a coping mechanism
- Language suggesting they're in immediate danger

Response: "I want to pause our session here. What you're describing sounds
like it goes beyond direction and into something that needs professional
support. Here are some resources..."

Do NOT continue the structured workflow. Do NOT try to be a therapist.
Do NOT minimize what they're expressing.
